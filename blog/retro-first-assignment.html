Retrospective: my first Coder Academy assignment

13 April 2023

It's done. My first assignment. Mixed emotions: relief, satisfaction, pride, frustration, stress - it's all there.

I thought this would be the "easy one" - I mean for starters, I had created a very similar site for my scholarship application in a similar timeframe! And that was all self-taught and without the guidance of instructors to teach me best practice. And yet that didn't even seem to be matter - I wanted this to be bigger, I wanted this to be better. I wanted to prove my worth.

The crushing weight of my own expectations was honestly the hardest part. I had to remember - I'm still learning. It doesn't have to be perfect. Heck, I can even keep changing and improving it whenever I want after the submission so that it's constantly evolving - how cool is that!

It's now 12 days after submission and I just received my mark back - 55.5/60. That's a HIGH DISTINCTION! I should be proud. And I am! But this assignment wasn't without it's challenges, that's for sure. Here's a retro on some of the things that this assignment taught me...

Presentations aren't my strength...

I have the gift of the gab and can make an excellent slide deck. My problem? Struggling to fit in everything I want to express and share, without stumbling over my words or going over time. Working remote for the last three years and learning to unmask my neurodivergencies haven't aided me in this task either... So what can I do, when presentations are going to be part of my future career in software? Firstly, even if I am often excellent at working under time pressure... don't leave recording your presentation until the submission date. I'm looking at you, ADHD...

I've glanced ahead at the requirements of our upcoming assignments and can see there's more presentations looming in the future, so I certainly want to be more mindful about when I attempt to record my presentation next time, so that I feel calm and prepared, and can workshop the delivery.

Commit early, commit often

Something that I admit took me a little too long to get my head around was how often you should commit to GitHub when working on your local machine. My commits ending up coming in batches every few days with pretty meaningless descriptions about what changes I actually made.

What I hope to get more comfortable with going forward is committing straight from VSCode, rather than having to open up the Terminal app for my commits. One step at a time I guess...

Semantics and accessibility can always be better

While I did put a lot of effort into my semantics and accessibility, this ended up being an area that I lost the most marks on my assignment - which goes to show how there are so many lenses to consider when it comes to best practice.

So, what next?

I've already implemented a change for one of the pieces of feedback I received that was a really simple fix, and I plan to revisit and make adjustments to my site based on the remainder of the feedback. The biggest one to consider is in relation to the colour scheme not having enough contrast on some pages, which will involve having to update my colour palette slightly.

Beyond my portfolio site, I'm now deep into learning Python and have my first Workbook assignment due next weekend. The assignment features a mixture of "intro to web" research questions, written questions on features of Python, and problem-solving questions using a combination of Python code, pseudocode and flowcharts. Always learning new things at the moment!
