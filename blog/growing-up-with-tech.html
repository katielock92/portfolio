Growing up with tech - the millennial experience

29 March 2023

I was born in 1992, which firmly places me as a millennial - despite a few pieces of Gen Z lingo occasionally slipping into my vocabulary. My exact year of birth however places me into a rare stage where only those who were born within a mere few years either side of me had the exact same technology at pivotal moments of their adolescence.

Despite being a "digital native" and being incredibly comfortable with technology before I was even a legal adult, I still have significant usage and memory of older technology from earlier in my childhood. As I enter into my career transition into tech, I thought it would be timely (and hilarious) to reflect on some of these technology memories that now feel incredibly dated, yet felt perfectly normal at the time.

Floppy discs

Before the cloud, before even USB sticks... floppy discs were our file sharing/portable storage of choice. I vividly remember my excitement when my parents bought me a 5-pack of brightly-coloured floppy discs in middle school when it was on the stationary list for our IT class. We would pack these chunky discs with us to insert into our computer and store a whopping 1.4MB of data - typically used on our MS Word, PowerPoint or Paint "projects". My masterpiece of work stored on this in Year 8 was a "stop-motion" animation created in MS Paint and Movie Maker, despite my terrible artistic skills.

Dial-up internet

"Muuuuum get off the phone, I need to go on MSN!" - a commonly heard phrase in the early 2000's. Our internet shared a line with our home phone which meant only one could be used at a time (and this is before the rise of mobile phones too...) My family was late to the party with getting broadband internet too, which meant until around 2008, my internet time was severely limited and connection speeds were terrible. We even kept a logbook of our connection times because our internet plan included 20 hours of internet... a month.

Fax machines

While still existing in a fleeting number of industries, fax machines seem irrelevant with the invention of modern internet technology. But for a while they were a key communication staple for when physical mail was too slow, but you needed something in writing. This printer-meets-telephone machine would start ringing like a phone, then whir up and print you something. My family never owned our own one except oddly enough in 2011 - because my summer job one year whilst in university required one to receive my orders from businesses who still relied upon them. The old technology was offset with the excitement of seeing an order appear physically in front of my eyes.

No smartphones

My first smartphone? Aged 16 or 17 (and it was rubbish too)... and my first mobile altogether? I was 12! That might seem wild to younger generations, but essentially nobody my age had a mobile until we started high school. And even then, it was some heavy black-and-white screened Nokia that only really allowed you to call, text or play Snake. It was kind of nice to have some level of disconnect from your peers, but looking back I absolutely love having the internet in my pocket for other life benefits. I got my first iPhone at 19 and prior to that, "mobile internet" was slow and expensive even on smartphones... meaning sometimes getting lost would happen without the convenience of a maps app at your fingertips.
